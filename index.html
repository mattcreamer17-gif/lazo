The frame
60Traditional code. Bank-feed matching, cap-table math, entity state, form generation.
30Rule-based logic. Categorization trees, jurisdiction-specific tax rules, investor-match heuristics.
10Real model work. Unstructured receipts, novel-vendor inference, contract Q&A where retrieval alone does not close the gap.

Most teams invert this ratio. The LLM bill scales faster than revenue and accuracy plateaus below the human specialist.

The interesting question is not whether AI belongs in the Lazo product. It clearly does.

You are running a US back office for more than a thousand LatAm founders, growing at double digits every month, and shipping AI assistants for data rooms and document Q&A. That is a lot of moving parts, most of which have to be right on a regulated deadline.

The interesting question is which parts of the stack should be model work, which parts are rule-based logic, and which parts are boring deterministic code that should never touch a prompt.

The Eduba frame

The short version: most AI problems are not AI problems. Our working view is 60/30/10. About sixty percent of the work inside a back-office product like Lazo is traditional code and database work. Thirty percent is rule-based logic. The last ten percent is actual model territory.

The work is deciding which piece belongs on which layer before the build, not after.

The paper behind the frame

Interpretable Context Methodology

Lazo is building an agent layer over a regulated document corpus. Tax filings, cap tables, contracts, investor data rooms. ICM names the filesystem pattern we use to make agent context auditable, versionable, and reproducible across every customer account.

Published in ACM TiiS. Repo public, MIT license.

Five questions we would open with

Bring one workflow inside Lazo that you wish felt cleaner.

  1. When a customer uploads a messy receipt stack, what percentage of the classification actually needs an LLM versus a rules table with vendor embeddings?
  2. Who owns the AI architecture at Lazo, and does that person also own the specialist throughput number?
  3. What breaks first as you go from 1,000 customers to 3,000: the human specialist bench, the AI latency, or the data room schema?
  4. When Series A candidates ask to see your data room structure, are you showing them a product or a services artifact?
  5. If you could drop one vendor from your own back office tomorrow, which one, and why is it still there?
